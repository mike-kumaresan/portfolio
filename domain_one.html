Domain I: Planning and Preparation


I consider planning to be an essential part of my responsibilities as a teacher, and I put considerable effort into taking the curriculum and making it accessible and interesting to our students. Two key aspects of my work in this area include having and developing a deep knowledge of the subject matter as well as a strong understanding of the diverse learners that are in my classroom. I design my instruction and assessments in light of these two aspects.

Office Hours

I use office hours and free periods as a key way to get to know my students and build relationships with them. Being in a more informal environment than the classroom helps students to open up and also allows me to have conversations with them and provide mentorship to them outside of the subject matter that I teach in class.

Rational Exponents and Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion (Math 433) [2019]

Below is my lesson plan for an Algebra class on the topic of rational exponents. The student handout begins with a "Do Now" section in which the students warm up with some problems whose solutions were studied earlier in the year. These problems motivate the discussion of rational exponents. This is followed by some exercises for them to complete on the topic of the lesson. Finally, there is an application problem for them to see how the mathematical theory can be used in a real-world context.

Designing New Course Content

Science Research [2020, 2021]

In the 2020-2021 academic year, I had the privilege of being given the Science Research course as part of my teaching assignment. I was given the task of developing content for Mathematics portion of the course. I decided to present some material on Linear Algebra, Regression Analysis and Statistics to our students. A unique element of my presentation was the incorporation of Python coding in the teaching. I chose these topics based on my experience of having worked in the scientific research industry and in higher education prior to coming to Scarsdale. I believe these are important skills for our science students to master in their education, and it is important for them to study both scientific theory and then apply it in the context of coding.

Digital Logic Design [2022]

At the end of the 2021-2022 academic year, I was able to complete a project that began 3 years ago. In 2019, I was awarded a CFI Grant to develop a new applied mathematics course (See Domain IV). The work following the award of the grant culminated in a new course offered to students in the Mathematics Department called "Digital Logic Design." The course covers the study and characterization of the electronic and logical properties of digital circuits. The following topics are covered in the course: theoretical and practical issues concerning design with combinational and sequential logic circuits, and programmable logic devices; number systems; boolean algebra, representation and simplification of Boolean functions, universal logic families; finite-state machines, state tables and state diagrams, flip-flops, counters, and registers. Course work involves individual and team projects in which digital circuits are designed and prototypes are constructed and tested on breadboards. The projects are used to assess technical writing, oral presentation, teamwork and project management skills.
